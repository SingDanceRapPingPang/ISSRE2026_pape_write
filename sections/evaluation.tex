\section{Experimental Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

This section evaluates MPTA-Repair on both benchmark and industrial
case studies. The evaluation follows the clock-bound repair setting
defined in Section~\ref{sec:approach}, where only numerical bounds in
guards and invariants are repairable. For comparison, we evaluate
MPTA-Repair against an adapted iterative TARTAR-style baseline
\cite{kolbl2019clock}.

\subsection{Evaluation Setup and Research Questions}

We evaluate MPTA-Repair on two datasets, summarized in
Table~\ref{tab:dataset-statistics}. The benchmark dataset includes nine
benchmark models derived from UPPAAL benchmark
\cite{behrmann2004tutorial} and the XtaBenchmarkSuite
\cite{farkas2018benchmarks}, such as Elevator and FDDI. The industrial
dataset constructed in this work contains five closely coupled NTA
case-study models: Gear Control System for automotive transmission
\cite{lindahl1998gear}, SAE RoR for autonomous-driving decisions
\cite{alves2021double}, Train Controller Alarm for railway emergency
timing \cite{gonzalez2024uppaal}, Train Gate Controller for
shared-resource concurrency \cite{alur1994theory}, and Pacemaker for
medical pacing \cite{jiang2010heart}. Compared with the benchmark
models, these case studies involve more tightly coupled timing
constraints across multiple properties.

\input{tables/dataset-statistics}

Since real-world NTA models with labeled clock-bound faults are
difficult to obtain, we synthesize faulty instances using mutation
\cite{jia2011mutation}. We retain only mutants that are syntactically
valid, violate at least one timed safety property, and preserve the
original functional behavior. Each repair instance is executed with a
10-minute timeout for both MPTA-Repair and the baseline.

We compare MPTA-Repair against an adapted iterative TARTAR-style
baseline \cite{kolbl2019clock}, which was originally designed for
single-property repair and operates via trace-local repair generation.
The evaluation is organized around the following research questions:

\noindent\textbf{RQ1.} What characteristics do the mutation-generated
repair instances exhibit, and how do the benchmark and industrial
datasets differ in model scale and multi-property complexity?

\noindent\textbf{RQ2.} How effectively does MPTA-Repair generate
accepted repairs compared with the iterative TARTAR-style baseline on
the benchmark and industrial datasets?

\noindent\textbf{RQ3.} To what extent does iterative space optimization
improve the repair efficiency of MPTA-Repair?

\noindent\textbf{RQ4.} What roles do full-property regression
verification and functional-equivalence admissibility checking play in
ensuring repair validity, and what are the main causes of unsuccessful
repairs?

\subsection{Results by Research Question}

\noindent\textbf{RQ1: Dataset and mutation characteristics.}
Table~\ref{tab:dataset-statistics} shows that the benchmark dataset
contains more original models, whereas the industrial dataset is larger
per model and has more properties per model. The benchmark models have
an average of 30.56 locations and 52.67 transitions, while the
industrial models have 57.80 locations and 91.80 transitions on
average. The retained mutants therefore cover both localized benchmark
faults and more tightly coupled industrial timing faults.

\noindent\textbf{RQ2: Repair effectiveness.}
Table~\ref{tab:overall-results} shows that MPTA-Repair outperforms the
iterative baseline on both datasets. On the benchmark dataset, the
baseline repairs 39 of 54 faulty instances, achieving a 72\% success
rate, whereas MPTA-Repair repairs 50 instances and reaches 93\%. The
gap is larger on the industrial dataset: the baseline repairs 21 of 105
instances, while MPTA-Repair repairs 80, improving the success rate from
20\% to 76\%. This result indicates that joint property-trace candidate
generation is especially useful when timing constraints and properties
are tightly coupled.

\input{tables/search-optimization-results}

\noindent\textbf{RQ3: Effect of iterative space optimization.}
Table~\ref{tab:search-optimization-results} compares MPTA-Repair with
and without search optimization. On the benchmark dataset, the average
time changes slightly from 15.74s to 15.46s because many faults are
already localized. On the industrial dataset, optimization reduces the
average time from 231.45s to 85.28s by reusing encoded obligations and
focusing solving on relevant property-trace constraints and repairable
bounds.

\noindent\textbf{RQ4: Validation and failure analysis.}
Full-property regression verification and functional-equivalence
admissibility checking are both necessary for valid multi-property
repair. A candidate generated from one TDT may satisfy the local
constraint but still violate another property, so MPTA-Repair requires
each candidate to satisfy the full property set. The admissibility check
then rejects behavior-breaking edits that satisfy timing properties only
by removing intended functional behavior.

Nevertheless, the framework can still fail to generate repairs in
certain cases when it fails to find an admissible candidate within the
time budget. This limitation is prominent in complex models like the
Pacemaker case study, where tightly coupled timing constraints expand
the search space, causing the solver to time out before finding a valid
candidate.
